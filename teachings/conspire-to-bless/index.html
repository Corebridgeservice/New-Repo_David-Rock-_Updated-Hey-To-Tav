A teaching

Conspire to Bless

The Oldest Conspiracy in the World

Perplexed marks the spot. When Scripture seems to contradict itself, the contradiction is not the wall. It is an invitation to dig together. We treat the perplexity as a gift, the places that confuse us as the places to discover God’s truth and walk it out in victory. The treasure waits on the other side of the discomfort.

The Two Hands

Have you ever known exactly what someone you love needed next, and said nothing? You could see the move that would help them. You had the words. And you swallowed them, because nudging someone toward a decision, even a good one, felt like handling something that did not belong in your hands.

Have you ever watched someone who is brilliant with people work a room, and felt two things at once? Admiration, because they read people the way musicians read music. And unease, because you have been on the receiving end of that skill. Somebody once understood you well enough to get something from you, and you did not figure it out until later. You know the temperature of a grasping hand. Once you have felt it, every skillful hand looks suspicious.

Have you ever picked up a book on persuasion or influence, gotten three chapters in, and put it down feeling like you needed to wash your hands? The techniques worked. That was the problem. They worked whether or not the person using them was good.

So you live with a strange split. God’s Word commands you to love the people around you, actively, deliberately, with everything you have. And yet the very skills that active love would need, reading people, choosing words carefully, moving someone toward action, all of it feels like manipulation in waiting. The command says go. The conscience says careful. Something is wrong here, and you have sensed it.

Most of us resolve the tension by going passive, and we dress the passivity in faithful clothes. “I’ll just pray about it.” “It’s not my place.” “God will work on them in His timing.” I know those sentences from the inside. I have said all three, and sometimes I meant them, and sometimes I was hiding. Hear me: every one of those sentences can be true. Prayer is real. Timing is real. There are seasons to be silent. But you already know the difference between the silence of wisdom and the silence of hiding, because you have felt the difference in your own chest. Sometimes “I’ll just pray about it” is a sentence we use so we never have to find out whether our full strength, fully deployed, on behalf of someone we love, is something God actually wants.

So here is the doorway question this teaching walks through. What if there is a way to bring every tool you have, every skill, every insight, every ounce of understanding you carry about a person, at full strength, into their life, and have it be the opposite of manipulation? What if the difference between the grasping hand and the open hand is not the skill at all, but something underneath the skill?

And what if God Himself has been doing exactly this since before the world began, and Scripture has a word for it that will surprise you?

The word is conspiracy.

The Dark Word

Let me show you the problem before I show you the treasure. In Scripture, conspiracy is the enemy’s word. Watch it work.

Joseph, seventeen years old, walks across a field toward his brothers, and the text says this:

“They saw him from a distance, and before he came near to them, they conspired against him to put him to death.” (Genesis 37:18)

The Hebrew verb there is nakal (H5230), to plot with deceit, to scheme craftily. It is the word of brothers becoming predators.

Now Psalm 2, the psalm of the raging nations:

“Why do the nations rage and the peoples plot in vain? The kings of the earth take their stand and the rulers take counsel together, against ADONAI and against His Anointed.” (Psalm 2:1-2)

Kings gathering in secret, breathing threats together, coordinating against God Himself. Everywhere you look in the Word, this is the shape of the thing. Absalom’s conspiracy against his father. The forty men who bound themselves with an oath to kill Paul. The council that gathered by night to condemn Yeshua. Conspiracy is what the wicked do in the dark. So why would anyone who loves God reach for that word and claim it?

That question is exactly the right kind of uncomfortable. This is where perplexed marks the spot. We do not push past the discomfort. We dig in it.

Start with the English word itself. Conspire comes from two Latin words, con and spirare, and together they mean to breathe together. That is all the word actually says. People close enough to share breath, united enough to move as one. Nothing in the word is dark. The darkness comes from what the breathers gather to do. The word is a room; the people in it decide what kind of room it is.

Now go deeper, into the Hebrew, because the Hebrew has been holding this treasure all along.

Look again at Psalm 2. “The rulers take counsel together.” The verb is yasad (H3245), to found, to fix, to sit down together in close deliberation. And the noun that grows from that root is one of the most beautiful words in the Hebrew Scriptures: sod (H5475), the intimate council, the circle of trusted ones, the conversation held close. The plotting kings of Psalm 2 are running a sod. A counterfeit one.

Because here is the verse that turns everything over:

“The secret counsel of ADONAI is for those who fear Him, and He makes His covenant known to them.” (Psalm 25:14)

The secret counsel. That is sod. God keeps a council. There is a circle of close deliberation at the center of reality, a room where the deepest purposes are spoken, and ADONAI invites people into it. Amos says it even more plainly:

“Indeed, the Lord ADONAI does nothing without revealing His secret counsel to His servants the prophets.” (Amos 3:7)

His sod, again. The prophets were not informers stationed outside a locked room. They were friends brought inside one.

Look at the picture the old letters paint. In Paleo Hebrew (the early form of the alphabet, where each letter is a picture), sod is spelled samekh, vav, dalet. Samekh is a shield, a support, the thing that protects. Vav is a peg or a nail, the connector that fastens one thing to another. Dalet is a door. Read it as a picture: something protected, fastened, behind a door. The council of ADONAI is a guarded room with a door in it. And Psalm 25:14 tells you exactly who the door opens for: those who fear Him.

If you sat with the first teaching in this series, you just felt two threads tie themselves together. The fear of ADONAI, the awe that comes from actually seeing whose hand is at work, is not just the beginning of wisdom. It is the doorway into the council. And notice what He does with the ones who walk through that door. “He makes His covenant known to them.” Fear and covenant, in one breath, in one verse. The reverence opens the room, and inside the room He hands you the covenant. The grammar of Teaching #1 was already walking here: from beholding to being bound, from hey to tav.

So the perplexity begins to resolve, and it resolves upward. Scripture does not hate conspiracy because gathering in close counsel is evil. Scripture exposes the counterfeit because the original is holy. The wicked conspire because they are imitating, in the dark, a room that actually exists in the light. The question was never whether there is a conspiracy at the center of the world. The question is what the true one is gathered to do.

So let us go find out.

The Counter-Conspiracy

Go back to that field where we left Joseph, seventeen and surrounded. The brothers’ plot worked, as plots often do. The pit, the caravan, the slave block, the prison. Decades pass. And then comes one of the most stunning sentences in the Hebrew Scriptures, spoken by Joseph to the very brothers who conspired against him:

“You meant evil against me, but God meant it for good, to bring about this present outcome, to keep many people alive.” (Genesis 50:20)

The Hebrew underneath that sentence is even better than the English. The verb on both sides is the same word: chashav (H2803), to weave, to plan, to devise. You wove evil against me. God wove it for good. Two looms, one thread. The brothers ran their dark little council in a field, and the whole time their scheme was a few threads inside a vastly older weaving, a plan whose stated purpose was to keep many people alive. The conspiracy against Joseph was real. It was also outmatched. There was a deeper conspiracy underneath it, running the opposite direction, and its agenda was rescue.

If that were the only case, we could call it a beautiful exception. It is not the only case. It is the pattern, and it runs all the way to the center of history.

Listen to the first believers praying in Jerusalem, quoting Psalm 2, the counterfeit-council psalm we just read, and hear what they say about the cross:

“For truly in this city there were gathered together against Your holy servant Yeshua, whom You anointed, both Herod and Pontius Pilate, along with the Gentiles and the peoples of Israel, to do whatever Your hand and Your counsel predestined to take place.” (Acts 4:27-28)

Read that slowly. The darkest conspiracy ever assembled, kings and governors and crowds breathing together to kill the Anointed One, and the praying believers say it accomplished what God’s hand and God’s counsel had marked out beforehand. The Greek word there is boule (G1012), counsel, deliberate purpose, the settled plan of a deliberating mind. It is the Greek cousin of sod. The counterfeit council gathered at night and did its worst, and every move it made fell inside the older council’s design. They wove a murder. He wove a redemption. Two looms, one thread, again.

So God’s council is real, it is older than its counterfeits, and it cannot be outmaneuvered. But what is it for? What is the room gathered to do? He told Abraham directly, in the founding charter of the whole covenant family:

“I will make you a great nation, and I will bless you and make your name great, and you will be a blessing. I will bless those who bless you, and whoever curses you I will curse, and in you all the families of the earth will be blessed.” (Genesis 12:2-3)

Bless, blessing, bless, bless, blessed. Five times in two verses. This is the agenda of the council at the center of reality, stated in plain covenant language: blessing, deployed through a chosen family, until it reaches every family on earth. And notice the shape of it. Abraham is not blessed as a terminus. He is blessed as a channel. “I will bless you… and you will be a blessing.” The blessing lands in his hands already moving, the way water lands in a riverbed.

Now hold the Hebrew word for bless up to the light, because the letters are about to paint the whole theology. The word is barak (H1288). It is family to berekh, the knee, and in its oldest usage it carries the sense of kneeling. In Paleo Hebrew, barak is spelled bet, resh, kaf. Bet is a house, the place of dwelling. Resh is a head, the highest, the chief. Kaf is an open palm. Read the picture: from the house of the Highest, by the open palm. That is what blessing is. And the kneeling buried in the root tells you how it travels: the Highest One bends down, lowers Himself to where you are, and puts something into your life with an open hand.

There are the two hands from the beginning of this teaching, hiding inside one Hebrew word. Manipulation is a grasping hand reaching toward you to take. Blessing is an open palm bending toward you to give. The skill of the hand was never the issue. The posture of the hand is everything.

Paul saw the whole design at once and could barely keep his sentences from overflowing:

“Blessed be the God and Father of our Lord Yeshua the Messiah, who has blessed us with every spiritual blessing in the heavenly places in Messiah, just as He chose us in Him before the foundation of the world.” (Ephesians 1:3-4)

Before the foundation of the world. The council to bless predates everything it blesses. It is not God’s response to the enemy’s plotting. The enemy’s plotting is a late, doomed imitation of a room that was already full.

Even the poets who never opened a concordance have caught the edge of this. John Keats, watching an English autumn ripen, wrote of the season and the sun as two friends scheming:

“Season of mists and mellow fruitfulness, Close bosom-friend of the maturing sun; Conspiring with him how to load and bless With fruit the vines that round the thatch-eves run”

Conspiring how to load and bless. The poet looked at creation in September and saw collaborators breathing together over how to be generous. He was more right than he knew. All truth is God’s truth, and the fruit on the vine is council business.

So here is where the dig has brought us. There is a conspiracy at the center of the world. It is older than its counterfeits, it cannot be outwoven, its agenda is blessing every family on earth, and its signature is an open palm bending down. God is the original Conspirator to Bless.

And Psalm 25:14 already told us the astonishing part: He opens the door. The council is recruiting.

Joining the Conspiracy

So what does joining look like? Not for a prophet, not for a patriarch with a covenant charter, but for you, on an ordinary Tuesday, with your actual family and your actual friends and the actual people who wear you out?

It looks like the second commandment, standing up and going to work.

The command was given at Sinai, in Torah (the whole counsel of His living Word), folded into a chapter about daily fairness, honest scales, and how you treat the people within arm’s reach:

“You shall not take vengeance, nor bear a grudge against the children of your people, but you shall love your neighbor as yourself. I am ADONAI.” (Leviticus 19:18)

And when a Torah scholar asked Yeshua to name the greatest commandment, He reached for love of God with everything, and then He reached straight back to this verse and refused to let it be second-class:

“And the second is like it: You shall love your neighbor as yourself.” (Matthew 22:39)

The Greek verb is agapao (G25), the love that chooses and acts and keeps choosing, love as commitment rather than mood. The word for neighbor is plesion (G4139), simply the one near you, whoever has come close. But the Hebrew underneath Yeshua’s quotation is the word worth slowing down for: re’a (H7453), the neighbor who is also the friend, the companion, the one your life actually touches. In Paleo Hebrew, re’a is spelled resh, ayin. Resh is a head, the highest. Ayin is an eye. Read the picture: the one you look at with the highest regard, the one whose eyes you learn to see through. The word itself tells you what the love requires. You cannot love your re’a from a distance, on principle, in the abstract. You have to get close enough to see what they see.

And then the commandment hands you the measuring stick: kamokha (H3644), as yourself. You already know how you want to be seen. Accurately, patiently, with your story taken into account. You already know how you want to be helped. Not managed, not steamrolled, not handled. Seen first, then served. The commandment says: that knowledge you carry about yourself is the standard. Now spend it on the person near you.

Here is the move this whole teaching has been walking toward. Put the second commandment together with the council. Love your neighbor as yourself, inside a conspiracy whose agenda is blessing every family on earth, with the open palm as your posture. There is a name for what that produces, and the name is the framework I have built my work and my relationships around. I call it Conspire to Bless™.

To conspire to bless someone is the deliberate, strategic, full-capacity deployment of every tool, framework, and capability at our disposal in service of moving someone toward action that makes their life and the lives around them measurably better.

Read that definition again, slowly, because every phrase in it is load-bearing. Deliberate: not accidental kindness, but planned goodness, schemed goodness, goodness with a calendar. Strategic: thought through, sequenced, patient. Full-capacity: nothing held back, every skill you were warned about now reporting for duty. Every tool: your insight into people, your way with words, your ability to read a room, your timing. In service of moving someone toward action: not warm feelings at a distance, but actual movement in an actual life. That makes their life and the lives around them measurably better: real fruit, visible in time, rippling outward past the person into everyone their life touches.

And now the question from the opening of this teaching, the one that split your conscience down the middle, finally gets its answer. What separates this from manipulation, when the tools are identical? Hold the two hands side by side one more time.

Manipulation says: I understand you well enough to get what I want from you.

Conspiring to bless says: I understand you well enough to help you get what you need.

Same understanding. Same skill. Same closeness. Opposite hands. The manipulator studies you the way a thief studies a house. The conspirator to bless studies you the way a craftsman studies the grain of the wood, looking for the way God already runs through you, so that every push lands with the grain and not against it. The skill was never the sin. The grasp was the sin. Open the hand, and the very abilities you have been afraid of become council equipment.

This is why the passive option, the spiritualized silence we named at the start, was never the holy choice it dressed up as. The commandment is active. The council is active. The Highest kneels and gives; He does not hover and hope. To love your re’a as yourself, with full capacity and an open palm, is to take your seat in the oldest conspiracy there is.

But a conspiracy this active needs an aim it can be measured by. Blessed toward what? And how would you know if your conspiring were actually blessing, rather than just busy? The council has a measure for that too. It is more demanding than you might expect, and it is the most beautiful part of the whole design.

The Measure

Abraham Joshua Heschel, one of the great Jewish theologians of the last century, carried a conviction that I have adopted as the ethical spine of everything I build. The greatness of a generation, he taught, is not judged by the contributions of its most brilliant thinkers in their ivory towers. It is judged by how high it elevates the person at the bottom: the one with the fewest resources, whether those resources are money, education, relationships, or spiritual inheritance. I call it the Heschel Standard, and I hold every project, every plan, and every conspiracy to bless up against it.

I did not adopt it because Heschel said it. I adopted it because the King already had. Listen to Yeshua describing the final accounting, the day every hidden council is opened:

“And the King will answer them, ‘Truly I say to you, to the extent that you did it to one of the least of these brothers of Mine, you did it to Me.’” (Matthew 25:40)

The King does not audit the generation’s monuments. He audits its treatment of the least-resourced person in the room, and He takes that treatment personally. The Heschel Standard is not a modern ethic retrofitted onto Scripture. It is the King’s own measure, stated in His own voice, about His own family.

And in the same chapter, in the same breath of teaching, Yeshua tells the story that explains what our capacities are even for. A master, leaving on a journey, entrusts his wealth to his servants, “to each according to his own ability” (Matthew 25:15). Different amounts, deliberately. The five-talent servant and the two-talent servant both trade faithfully with what was placed in their hands, and they receive the identical commendation:

“Well done, good and faithful servant. You were faithful over a few things; I will set you over many things. Enter into the joy of your master.” (Matthew 25:21)

The master never measures the size of the entrustment. He measures what the open hand did with it. Capacity, in the economy of this kingdom, is stewardship, not status. Yeshua says it without a wasted word elsewhere: “From everyone who has been given much, much will be required” (Luke 12:48). If you can read people, that skill was issued to you. If you can find the right word at the right moment, issued. If you understand how someone’s heart works well enough to move it, issued, and the Issuer has an intended destination for every gift He placed in your hands.

Paul tells us the destination. Writing to a congregation at Corinth that was busy ranking its members by giftedness, he describes how the Designer actually engineered the body:

“On the contrary, the parts of the body that seem to be weaker are indispensable, and on those parts of the body that we think less honorable we bestow the greater honor… But God has so composed the body, giving greater honor to the part that lacked it, that there may be no division in the body, but that the members may have the same care for one another.” (1 Corinthians 12:22-25)

Read that as an engineering specification, because that is what it is. God did not design the body so that honor pools at the top. He composed it, deliberately, so that honor flows toward the member who lacked it. The strong exist for the weak the way the trellis exists for the vine. That is not charity bolted onto the design. That is the design.

Now watch all the threads pull tight at once. The word barak carries a kneeling inside it: the Highest bends down to bless. The body is composed so honor runs downhill to whoever has the least. The King measures every generation, every family, every life by what happened to the least of these. Do you see it? The direction of blessing, everywhere in this Word, is downward and then upward: the open palm lowers itself so that someone else can rise. So the measure of your conspiring is not how impressive it looks from above. It is how high it lifts from below.

Which means the Heschel Standard turns every room you walk into upside down, in the best way. The question is no longer “who here can do something for me?” That is the grasping hand’s question, and you can feel its temperature from across the room. The question becomes “who here is carrying the fewest resources, and what would it take to elevate them?” The new believer with no inheritance of Scripture. The teenager nobody asks real questions. The man whose confidence was spent decades ago. The woman at the table who has stopped expecting to be noticed. Blessed toward what? Toward that person standing taller. Measured how? By whether they actually rise.

That is the aim of the council you have been invited to join. Which leaves only the practical question, the one the warm father always gets to last on purpose: what do we actually do, this week, with the people at our own table?

The Practice

Start small, start close, and start this week. A conspiracy to bless does not begin with a program. It begins with a person.

Pick your person the way the King picks. Run the Heschel question over the rooms you are already in. Your home, your work, your congregation, your street. Who is carrying the fewest resources right now? Not who is easiest to help, and not who will be most grateful. Who has the least? Start there.

Study them like a craftsman, not a thief. Get close enough to see through their eyes, because that is what loving your re’a actually requires. What has God already placed in them that nobody has named out loud? What do they need next, not in general, but actually? What would they say they need, if anyone asked them and then stayed quiet long enough to hear the whole answer? This is where every skill you own goes to work: your attention, your questions, your memory, your timing. Full capacity, open palm.

Then conspire, in the literal sense: breathe together. A conspiracy takes more than one. Bring someone into it. A spouse, one of your children, a friend. Sit down together and plan goodness with a calendar on the table. What are we going to do for this person, when, and in what order? Who says the word that needs saying? Who provides the thing that needs providing? There is a particular joy that shows up in a family scheming together over how to make someone’s life measurably better, and I am convinced that joy is the family discovering it has council blood in it.

Test the hand before you move. One question, asked honestly, keeps the whole practice clean: am I trying to get something from this person, or for them? If the answer drifts toward from, stop and let the Father reset your palm. If the answer is for, move with all your strength.

Aim at them standing taller, not at you feeling generous. The blessing is not complete when you have given something. It is complete when they have risen. Watch for it. The Heschel Standard is patient, but it does insist on fruit you can see.

And parents, hear one more thing underneath all of this. Your children are growing up into the most skillfully engineered persuasion any generation has ever faced. Wave after wave of grasping hands, polished to look like open ones, are already reaching for their attention, their identity, their hearts. You cannot wall them off from every counterfeit. But there is an older defense, and it is the one the Word has been showing us this whole teaching: the way to recognize a counterfeit is to know the original deeply. A child who has sat inside a real conspiracy to bless, who has schemed goodness at the dinner table and watched somebody rise because of it, carries a reference point in their chest for the rest of their life. They will know the temperature of a grasping hand the moment they feel it, because they grew up holding an open one.

Closing

So here is what I want to leave with you. Two hands have been reaching through this whole teaching. One grasps. One opens. Both are skillful, and the skill was never the question. The question was always which council the hand belongs to.

You were spoken into being by a God who blessed before He commanded, who kneels in the very letters of His own word for blessing, who out-wove the darkest conspiracy in history and turned it into rescue, and who keeps a council with a door in it. The fear of ADONAI opens that door. Inside it, He shows you the covenant, and He hands you your assignment: love the one near you, as yourself, at full capacity, until the person with the least stands tall.

So look at your own hands this week. Where has the fear of being manipulative kept them closed? Whose name came to mind while you read about the person carrying the least? Do not push past that. Perplexity marked the spot at the start of this teaching, and a name surfacing in your heart marks the spot now. Stop. Ask the Father what He is already weaving for that person. Dig. And then take your seat at the table where the oldest conspiracy in the world is still meeting, still scheming, still loading the vines to bless.

The door is open. The council is recruiting. Bring your whole hand.

~ David
