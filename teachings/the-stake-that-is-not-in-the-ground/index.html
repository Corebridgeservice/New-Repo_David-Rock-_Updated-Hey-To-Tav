The Stake That Is Not in the Ground

Two Stakes, One Surrender

I cannot think unless I have been thought,

Nor can I speak unless I have been spoken.

I cannot teach except as I am taught,

Or break the bread except as I am broken.

O Mind behind the mind through which I seek,

O Light within the light by which I see,

O Word beneath the words with which I speak,

O founding, unfound Wisdom, finding me,

O sounding Song whose depth is sounding me,

O Memory of time, reminding me,

My Ground of Being, always grounding me,

My Maker’s Bounding Line, defining me,

Come, hidden Wisdom, come with all you bring,

Come to me now, disguised as everything.

Malcolm Guite, “O Sapientia,” from Sounding the Seasons (Canterbury Press, 2012).

Read it slowly. Read it twice.

The sonnet sets the posture for everything that follows. I cannot teach except as I am taught. Or break the bread except as I am broken. What lies ahead in this piece is bread being broken into a new identity, a wilderness humbling that fed a people on every word from His mouth, and a hidden Wisdom that has been waiting under every perplexing passage for those willing to come close enough to find Him there. Come, hidden Wisdom, come with all you bring, come to me now, disguised as everything. That is the posture. Now we begin.

Perplexed marks the spot. When Scripture seems to contradict itself, the contradiction is not where the road ends. It is an invitation to dig together. We treat the perplexity as a gift, the places that confuse or feel off as the places to discover God’s truth and walk His truth out in greater victory. The treasure is hidden just below the surface, on the other side of the discomfort of perplexity.

A brief note on what this is and what this is not. This piece is not an exhaustive study of every verse on identity. It is “concise” on purpose. A few key passages are quoted, a handful of Greek words are unpacked with their Strong’s reference numbers so you can look them up for yourself, and the rest of the dig is left to you. Receive what lands here if it does. If something calls to you to investigate further, follow that invitation. The Holy Spirit is a better study partner than any teacher. Follow His lead.

Are You Struggling?

Before we get to the story, I want to ask you a few questions. Sit with them. They are not rhetorical.

Are you struggling in your life right now because it does not look the way you expected it to look? You have been a believer for years. You love God. You read His Word. And yet some of the promises that fill the pages of your Bible seem to fall flat in your lived experience. The blessings sound real on paper. They feel distant in the morning. The victory described in the New Testament feels like someone else’s life.

Are you feeling discouraged? Are you feeling overwhelmed by things you used to handle with relative ease? Are you, maybe quietly, even feeling bitter toward God Himself, because the promises in Scripture do not seem to be materializing in your life the way you expected, or even the way you see them showing up in the lives of people around you?

If any of that is hitting close to home, please know that you are not alone. There is a reason for it, and the reason may not be what you think.

Open the book of Acts. Read chapter two and watch the Holy Spirit fall on a frightened band of fishermen until three thousand people are baptized in a single afternoon and the city around them turns inside out. Read chapter five and watch the apostles, freshly beaten by the Sanhedrin, walking out of the council rejoicing that they were counted worthy to suffer shame for His name (Acts 5:41). Read chapter seven and watch Stephen, full of grace and the Holy Spirit, looking up into heaven while the stones are already in the air. He is praying for the men who are killing him to be forgiven (Acts 7:60). He is naming aloud what he is seeing: the Son of Man standing at the right hand of God. He is dying without the line cracking. Read chapter sixteen and watch Paul and Silas, locked in stocks with their backs in bloody ribbons, singing hymns at midnight until the prison shook. The other prisoners are listening to the songs. The earthquake comes. The chains fall off. The jailer who would have killed himself out of duty falls at their feet asking how to be saved. He is taking them to his house at two in the morning to wash the blood off their backs and feed them. Read the gospels. Watch men and women who walked with so much grace and so much power that their lives reorganized cities. Then look at your own week. Watch yourself endure far less hardship with far less grace. Watch yourself shrink from much smaller pressures. Watch yourself say things to yourself in private moments that you would never say to someone you loved.

Something is wrong here.

This is the kind of moment perplexed marks the spot exists for. There is a contradiction between the lives the New Testament shows you and the life you are actually walking. Most believers feel it. Most believers never investigate it. They explain it away with one line they have heard so often, said by so many people, in such humble-sounding tones, that they never thought to question it.

I’m just a sinner saved by grace.

That line feels honest. It even feels holy. But what if it is also the shelter you keep stepping into whenever the gap between what God promises and what you are actually walking in becomes too uncomfortable to sit with? What if it is a quiet cop-out, the spiritual equivalent of I guess this is just my limit, dressed up in religious language so it does not look like resignation? What if the reason it feels safe is not because it is true, but because it lets you off the hook of having to ask why the gospel does not seem to be producing what it says it will?

What if it is not actually true?

What if the reason the same battles keep playing out the same way, year after year, is not that you are weak or that God is distant, but that somewhere in your young life as a believer you concluded a thing about yourself, and the conclusion has been outlasting the conditions that produced it ever since? What if you inherited something the enemy embedded as a tradition handed down through the Body, and what you have been living out in real time is your own faith in that conclusion?

Yeshua said it this way: according to your faith be it unto you (Matthew 9:29). So it has been.

What if it is not supposed to be like this?

What if you have come to believe a lie?

What if you are not actually the kind of creature you have been telling yourself you are?

Why This Piece Exists (before we get going)

I want to tell you a little about where this piece comes from.

Last week I spoke with a friend who is going through something hard. He is younger than I am, wired in many of the ways I am wired, sitting with real grief and the kind of developmental tunnel a man passes through in his own season. As we talked, something started to emerge that he had not directly named. Underneath what he was wrestling with was a question about identity. About who he was now in light of everything that was hurting him, everything he was wrestling with.

This is one of the things friendship is for. As iron sharpens iron, so one person sharpens another (Proverbs 27:17). As water reflects the face, so one heart reflects another (Proverbs 27:19). God uses friends as mirrors. He surfaces in conversation the lies we have been carrying that we could not have seen on our own. What my friend was actually holding came up to where both of us could see it. This was a relational version of perplexed marks the spot. The dissonance between who God says he is and the conclusion he had quietly made about himself rose to be seen, and the seeing was an invitation to dig.

There is something deeper underneath those proverbs that has shaped how I understand what I am doing in writing this at all.

The Hebrew word for brother is ach (אח). Two letters. Aleph and chet. Dr. Frank Seekins reads the ancient pictographs this way: aleph was a picture of an ox head, meaning strong; chet was a picture of a fence, meaning to surround and protect. Two letters together: strong fence.

This is what a brother is in the language God Himself chose. He is the One doing the defining. The power, the freedom, the depth of true brotherhood all live in His definition, not in our biology. A brother is a strong fence around a soul, a function that keeps the predator out and keeps the sheep in. A person can share your DNA and not function as a fence in your life. A person can share none of your DNA and stand as a strong fence around you.

Watch this in the most famous brother story in the Bible. When God asks Cain where his brother Abel is, Cain answers with a question that has echoed through every generation since. Am I my brother’s keeper? (Genesis 4:9). The Hebrew word for keeper is shamar (שמר), to guard, to watch, to protect. The horror of Cain’s question is that the answer is already inside the noun brother he just used. To be a brother IS to be a keeper. The fence is what the word means.

Yeshua described the same architecture in the sheepfold (John 10:1-10). First-century sheepfolds were stones with thorny branches piled on top. There was one entrance, and the shepherd would lie across it, his own body becoming the door. Anyone climbing in by another way was a thief by definition. I am the door of the sheep (John 10:7). He is the strong fence in His own person.

This is what I am attempting to be for my friend, regardless of the blood relation we do not share. I am trying to keep the predator out, which is the lie the enemy has been working to embed in his identity, and keep the sheep in, which is the truth God has spoken over him. Being a strong fence is not always comfortable. Sometimes it requires speaking truth in love when consoling words might feel kinder. False comfort that confirms a lie is not compassion. It is abdication of a brother’s duty to, imperfectly, strive to be a strong fence.

So the intention behind this piece is to be a strong fence. It is also a model for what I want to invite you into. The friends you will read this with in your own life are ones you can be a strong fence for. We are designed by Him to function this way for each other. He is the One defining brotherhood. We are the ones who get to live it out.

That is what I was holding when I sat with my friend.

He did not say I’m just a sinner saved by grace in those exact words. That articulation came later, in an Instagram reel he sent me as part of an ongoing exchange about identity. But the philosophy was already operating in him, and the conversation brought it into the light.

In the moment I was torn between letting him vent, comforting him, and confronting the lie I discerned at the root of what he was wrestling with. Sometimes this is the blessing struggle brings: enough pain to finally dig deep enough to reach the root. I could not let it stand unchecked. I challenged what I sensed was being revealed in him. He needed truth more than reassurance.

That conversation is why this is on a page in front of you. I wrote what follows because I love my friend and wanted him to walk out of this struggle with something better than the line he had been carrying. I also wrote it because, sitting with him, I realized the same trap was still operating in places of my own life. He has been speaking things over me for a while that I have not been willing to fully receive, because some part of me kept defaulting back to I’m not ready yet, I’m not enough yet, this is my limit. My own quieter version of the same conclusion this piece is walking through. It is not the limit. And I am still walking that truth out. The piece you are about to read is for my friend. It is also for me. And now it is for you.

If you have been carrying the same line, what follows is here to help you set it down.

The Elephant and the Stake

You have probably heard the story. It keeps showing up in talks because it carries a truth most people sense but cannot quite name.

When a young elephant is taken into captivity, the trainers drive a stake into the ground and tie one of its legs to it with a rope. The elephant pulls. The stake holds, because the elephant is small and the rope is tight and the ground has been chosen by men who know the limits of a young elephant’s strength. After enough days the elephant stops pulling. It learns something about itself. It learns that the stake holds. It learns that the rope wins. It learns that it cannot leave.

Years later, the same elephant weighs six tons. Same rope. Same stake. He could uproot the stake by leaning to one side. He could shake the rope off by twitching his leg. He could walk away in any direction he chose.

He does not.

He stays inside the small circle the rope once defined. Not because the rope holds him. The rope is no longer strong enough to hold him. He stays because somewhere in his young life he concluded a thing about himself, and the conclusion outlasted the conditions that produced it. The stake does not hold him. The story he learned about himself holds him.

The story gets told because it points at something true. Not about elephants. About us. Hold on to that stake, by the way. It is the first of two you will meet in this piece, and the second one changes everything.

The Conclusion Most of Us Inherited

Ask a thousand Christians who they are, and a lot of us will say something like this: I’m just a sinner saved by grace.

We say it humbly. We mean it as a confession of dependence. We learned to say it in Sunday school and from sermons and from worship songs. It threads through our prayers. Lord, I am just a wretched sinner, but for your mercy. We hear it from pulpits we trust. We hear it in books written by people we admire. We hear it from each other in small groups when someone is being honest about a struggle.

It is the foundation many of us stand on.

And whatever you build on a crooked foundation comes out crooked. On this foundation, almost all of us have quietly concluded that being a sinner is what we are. Not what we used to be. What we are now. Sinner is the noun. Saved by grace is the modifier. The noun claims your identity. The modifier only describes what happened to it.

This is the conclusion most believers made about themselves in their spiritual infancy. It was made under conditions we could not have understood at the time. It has outlasted the conditions. It is the stake the rope is tied to. And like our elephant, we keep pulling against it. We confess again. We try again. We promise to do better. And we never quite leave the small circle the rope once defined, because we keep believing the rope still holds.

But there is a verse, sitting in plain sight in the New Testament, that says something else entirely about what we are. And it has been there the whole time.

The Verse That Changes Everything

Here it is:

“Therefore, if anyone is in Messiah, he is a new creation. The old has passed away; behold, the new has come.”

(2 Corinthians 5:17)

That is Paul writing to a congregation about what happens to a person who is in Messiah. He does not say the new is on its way. He does not say the new will come if you try hard enough. He says the new has come. He does not say the old is fading. He says the old has passed away. Past tense. Already done.

Paul was a Hebrew of Hebrews, trained under Gamaliel, thinking in the categories of the Hebrew Scriptures even as this letter comes down to us in Greek. And in that Greek text, the words passed away sit in a verb tense that means completely finished, no further action needed. The words has come sit in a different tense that means it happened, and the result of it is still here now. So one sentence is saying: the old: done, gone, over. The new: arrived, present, here.

He also chose a particular Greek word for new. The word he picked is not the one you would use for a newer model of an old thing. There were two ways to say new in the Greek. One of them meant more recent in time. The other meant different in kind. Paul chose different in kind. The word he used was kainos (Strong’s G2537).

Imagine the difference between a phone that just got a software update and a phone that runs an entirely new operating system. The first is the same kind of device, refreshed. The second is something else, even if it looks similar on the outside. Paul is saying the believer is the second one. Not a refreshed version of his old self. A different kind of being entirely.

The believer is not a sinner who has been spruced up by grace. The believer is a new kind of creature, made by the same God who said let there be light and made a universe out of nothing. That same creative speech-act, that same kind of bringing-something-into-existence, is what happened in you when you were placed in Messiah Yeshua. The Greek word Paul uses here for creation is ktisis (Strong’s G2937), the same kind of word used in the Torah (the whole counsel of His living Word) for what God did in Genesis 1.

If this is true, the elephant’s rope is not tied to anything anymore. The ground he stands on is not the ground he was tied to when he was small. The whole location of his identity has been moved.

So why are so many of us still pulling against a stake that is no longer in the ground?

But What About These Other Verses?

I can almost hear the pushback. That sounds too clean. There are verses where Paul calls himself a sinner. There are verses that say even Christians are still sinners. You can’t just lift one verse and ignore the rest. That is fair. This is where perplexed marks the spot. The verses seem to fight each other; that fight is the doorway, not the wall. Let us walk through it together.

1. “Of whom I am chief.”

“This saying is faithful and worthy of all acceptance, that Messiah Yeshua came into the world to save sinners, of whom I am chief.”

(1 Timothy 1:15)

On a first read, this sounds like Paul saying he is the worst sinner alive, right now, in the present tense. Many people read it that way. If Paul felt this way about himself, the argument goes, surely we should too.

But look at what the Greek word translated chief actually means. It is the word prōtos (Strong’s G4413). It does not mean the worst. It means first, foremost, the original example, the prototype. Paul is not saying he is the most morally degraded person on the planet. He is saying he is the prototype of how mercy works. Exhibit A. The first model of what God’s grace can do.

The very next verse settles it. Paul writes:

“However, for this reason I obtained mercy, that in me first Messiah Yeshua might show all longsuffering, as a pattern to those who are going to believe on Him for everlasting life.”

(1 Timothy 1:16)

The word translated pattern in Greek is hypotypōsis (Strong’s G5296). It means outline, sketch, prototype. Paul is saying: look at me. I am the pattern. I am the first sketch of how mercy works. Anybody else who comes after me can see what God did with me and know he can do that for them.

He is not naming his present identity. He is naming his historical position. He is the head of the line of those Jesus came to save, and he keeps that history alive as a memorial so the rest of us know what mercy looks like at full scale. Read the verses just before this one. Paul says he was (past tense) a blasphemer. He says he obtained (past tense) mercy. He says grace was (past tense) exceedingly abundant. Past, past, past. The only present-tense verb in the whole passage is I am first, and the next sentence defines what first means: first as the prototype, not first as the resident of sin.

2. “While we were yet sinners.”

“But God demonstrates His own love toward us, in that while we were still sinners, Messiah died for us.”

(Romans 5:8)

This is the second verse people point at. On a quick read, it sounds like we are still sinners now. But look at the verb tense in the verse itself. The English says while we were still sinners. Past tense. Not while we are still sinners. Not because we are still sinners. While we were.

The whole point of this verse is the timing of the cross. God’s love is shown by when He acted. He did not wait until we cleaned up before sending His Son. He came while we were still in the old life, before we had any way of fixing ourselves, and pulled us out.

The argument in Romans goes like this. Chapter 5 contrasts the old humanity in Adam with the new humanity in Messiah. Chapter 6 says we were baptized into the death of Messiah and raised to walk in newness of life. Chapter 7 names how badly it goes when we try to live by Torah without the Spirit. Chapter 8 opens with a sentence that should be on every believer’s mirror:

“There is therefore now no condemnation to those who are in Messiah Yeshua.”

(Romans 8:1)

That whole arc of Romans 5 through 8 is Paul tracing the believer’s exit from the sinner-state into the in-Messiah state. To take Romans 5:8 and turn it into proof that we are still sinners now is to read one verse against the whole chapter, the whole section, and the whole letter.

3. “O wretched man that I am.”

“O wretched man that I am! Who will deliver me from this body of death?”

(Romans 7:24)

This is the third verse. The wretched man passage. Romans 7 is Paul describing the experience of trying to live by Torah without the Spirit. The chapter is a portrait of bondage. The chapter ends with the cry for rescue.

But here is the trick most people miss. The very next sentence is the answer:

“There is therefore now no condemnation to those who are in Messiah Yeshua, who do not walk according to the flesh, but according to the Spirit.”

(Romans 8:1)

Paul writes the wretched-man cry, and then writes the answer in the next breath. Romans 7 is the before. Romans 8 is the after. The wretched man is the trapped self trying to obey the Torah in his own strength. The no-condemnation self is the one who has been freed and is now walking in the Spirit.

To take Romans 7:24 as a description of how the believer should think about himself is to read a captured-man verse onto a freed man and refuse to read the next paragraph. The wretched man was Paul before. The new creation is Paul now.

None of these three verses say what people who quote them propose that they mean. Paul is consistent across all his letters. Past sin, present new creation, in Messiah, no condemnation. That is the architecture, the choreography of truth in sequence.

The Wilderness Has Seen This Story Before

The trap our elephant is in is not new. It is ancient. There is a moment in the Hebrew Scriptures where the same trap caught a whole nation, and the consequences fell on an entire generation.

Not long before this scene, God had brought the people of Israel out of slavery in Egypt. He had led them through the wilderness. At Sinai He had given them the Torah. He had promised them a land. Now they had arrived at its border. The land was wide and rich, and it was theirs by promise. All they had to do was walk in.

Before going in, they sent twelve spies to look at the land. The spies came back. Two of them said let us go up at once and take possession of it, for we are well able to overcome it. The other ten said something different. They had seen the people who lived in the land. They had seen the giants. And then they said one of the most devastating sentences ever recorded about how a people sees itself:

“There we saw the giants, the descendants of Anak, who come from the giants; and we were like grasshoppers in our own sight, and so we were in their sight.”

(Numbers 13:33)

We were like grasshoppers in our own sight. The grasshopper-conclusion came first. The way they saw themselves came first. The way the giants saw them was downstream of the way they saw themselves. Their self-account opened the door for the world’s account of them.

The land was already theirs by promise. God had said it. Moses had repeated it. They had been brought to the very edge of it. And then ten men brought a report from man, based on what their eyes had seen. Two men, Joshua and Caleb, brought a report from God, anchored to what He had already spoken. The people had to choose which report to agree with. They agreed with the ten. The man-report won the vote. The God-report was rejected. The door closed on that generation. Every man of fighting age at that moment was sentenced to wander in the wilderness until he died (Numbers 14:29). One whole generation slowly dying out in the dust, the inheritance only a few miles away the whole time. Their children, the ones who had been too young to vote with the spies, were the ones who eventually crossed the Jordan under Joshua. The only thing between that generation and the inheritance was a self-perception they refused to update.

The grasshopper was the elephant of the wilderness. The land was on the other side of the conclusion they would not change. They could not take what was already theirs because they would not change what they believed about themselves.

Notice what makes their failure so devastating. It was not only that they refused to believe what they could be. They refused to believe what God had already shown them and said to them. This was the same God who had brought them out of slavery with signs and wonders no people had ever witnessed. The same God who had parted the sea and drowned the army of Pharaoh behind them. The same God who had fed them in the wilderness with bread from heaven. The same God who had spoken to them out of the cloud and the fire on Mount Sinai and given them His Torah with His own voice. The same God who had said, in the plainest possible terms, I have given you this land. Walk in. They had experienced His power in their bodies and in their history. They had heard His voice with their own ears. And in the moment when His promise required them to act on what He had said, they looked at themselves, they looked at the giants, they looked away from everything He had already done, and they said we are grasshoppers.

It was not only an identity failure. It was a failure of faith in Him. The grasshopper-conclusion was the symptom. The deeper trouble was that they did not trust His word, against the evidence of His finished works on their behalf, when the moment came to walk it out.

The parallel for us is exact. We have read the Good News of what He did on the cross. We have read what He sent in His Holy Spirit. Many of us have experienced His presence in unmistakable ways, in healing, in deliverance, in answered prayer, in moments when His nearness was as undeniable as a hand on the shoulder. We have the witness of generations of His Body walking in His power. And yet, when His word about who we now are meets our actual self-image in the morning, we do the same thing the wilderness generation did. We look at ourselves. We look at the giants in our actual week. We look away from everything He has already done. And we say, I’m just a sinner saved by grace.

It is not only an identity failure. It is a failure of faith in Him. In what He has said. In what He has finished. In what He has empowered us to walk in. And He said this:

“Amen, amen, I say to you, the one believing in Me, the works that I do, that one will do also, and greater than these he will do, because I am going to the Father.”

(John 14:12)

Read that line again. Not metaphorical. Not someday in glory. Here. Now. Those who believe in Him do the works He did, and greater. Not necessarily greater in singular significance, though sometimes that. Certainly greater in volume, because there are countless of us, all of us empowered by His Holy Spirit, walking in His authority into rooms He cannot physically enter Himself in this age. We were designed for this. We were rebuilt for this. The grasshopper-mind cannot do this. The new-creation mind, standing on His word, can.

The wilderness generation died in the dust within sight of the inheritance because they could not bring themselves to trust His word above their own assessment of themselves. We face the same question every morning. Will we trust His word about us above our own assessment of ourselves? Will we walk in what He has said is true, even when our feelings disagree and the giants in our week look real?

The enemy reads this passage too. He knows what self-perception costs a people. He knows that the most effective form of bondage is one the captive does not need anyone to enforce. A rope tied to a stake that is no longer in the ground will hold a six-ton elephant for the rest of his life if he believes it still holds.

The Day That Became a Curse, and Will Become a Blessing

There is something else about that day I want you to see, because the consequences of agreeing with the man-report did not stop at the wilderness generation. They left a mark on the calendar.

Ancient Jewish tradition (Mishnah Ta’anit 4:6) records that the spies returned and the people fell into mourning on the ninth day of the Hebrew month of Av. The Jewish calendar has carried that date as a day of mourning ever since, called Tisha B’Av (the ninth of Av). For more than two thousand years it has been kept as the saddest day on the Jewish calendar, a fast day of mourning observed in Jewish communities around the world, when the scroll of Lamentations is read aloud and the destruction of the Temple is grieved. What lands on that same calendar date, or within the same week of it, century after century, is staggering.

The First Temple, Solomon’s Temple, was destroyed by the Babylonians on Tisha B’Av. The Second Temple was destroyed by the Romans on Tisha B’Av, more than five hundred years later, on the same day of the calendar. The Bar Kokhba revolt was crushed by Rome on Tisha B’Av. The Romans plowed Jerusalem on the same day. The Jewish community of England was expelled by King Edward I on Tisha B’Av in 1290. The Jewish community of Spain was given its final deportation deadline on Tisha B’Av in 1492. World War I began with German and Russian mobilizations on Tisha B’Av in 1914. Hermann Göring’s order to Reinhard Heydrich to prepare the Final Solution was signed two days before Tisha B’Av in 1941, in the same week as the date. The mass deportations from the Warsaw Ghetto to the Treblinka death camp began on Tisha B’Av in 1942. On that same day, Adam Czerniaków, the head of the Warsaw Jewish council, took his own life rather than sign the orders demanding the deportation of the ghetto’s children. His final diary entry that morning read: They demand that I kill the children of my people with my own hands. There is nothing for me to do but die. The list goes on.

This is not coincidence. For some of these events the biblical or historical record gives a date a day or two off, landing within the same week as Tisha B’Av rather than precisely on it; Jewish tradition has synthesized the cluster onto the ninth of Av because the pattern across centuries was too strong to read any other way. Jewish tradition holds that the day the people agreed with the man-report over the God-report became the day on which the people kept paying for the agreement. Calamity after calamity, on the same calendar date, across thousands of years. The consequence of agreeing with the man-report did not stop at the wilderness generation. It became a date on the calendar.

And yet hold this. Jewish tradition also holds that Tisha B’Av will be redeemed. The prophet Zechariah said it this way: the fast of the fifth month shall become joy and gladness, and cheerful feasts to the house of Judah (Zechariah 8:19). The fifth month is Av. The day of deepest mourning is appointed to become a day of joy and rejoicing. The curse will be reversed. The God who allowed Israel to bear the weight of its agreement with the ten has already promised to redeem the same date.

So what is this for you?

It is this. When God has shown you who He is, when He has done things in your life and in your generation that only He could do, when He has spoken His report over who you now are, and you then agree with the whispered report of the enemy instead, you are stepping into the same architecture that turned a calendar date into a curse for centuries. The accountability is exponential. The God who said I have given you this land will not let you keep agreeing with we are grasshoppers without consequence. He loves you too much to let you live indefinitely in your own opposition to His word.

And the inverse is just as true. The God who has promised to redeem the ninth of Av is the same God who is offering to redeem every day you have spent in agreement with the lie. Repent. Stop eating the report of the ten. Listen to the two. Joshua and Caleb were standing right there the whole time, with a different report anchored in what God had said. The ratio of the spies, ten to two, was never the load-bearing variable. The load-bearing variable was which report the people agreed with. The same is true for you now. The ratio of whispered lies to spoken truths in your own head is irrelevant. The only question is which report you are choosing to agree with. Choose the two.

You Have Come of Age

Before we leave the wilderness, there is one more detail in the story I want you to slow down for, because the same God who allowed a whole generation to fall in the dust did something else in the same sentence that most readers walk past.

When God sentenced the spies’ generation to die in the wilderness, He named the line precisely. Numbers 14:29: In this wilderness your bodies shall fall, all of you who were numbered, according to all your number, from twenty years old and upward, who have grumbled against Me. The cutoff was twenty. Every man twenty and older at Kadesh, who had been counted in the census of the fighting age, was the one held accountable. The under-twenty were not.

Numbers 14:31 names what He did instead with the under-twenty: But your little ones, whom you said would be a prey, I will bring in, and they shall know the land which you have rejected. The children whose parents had refused the land were the ones God brought in. The very land their parents lost, He gave to them. The mercy is staggering when you sit with it.

The principle underneath is older than the wilderness. God draws a line between the one who chose against Him with knowledge of what he was doing and the one who was carried along by a generation he did not pick. Deuteronomy 1:39 says it plainly: your little ones, who you said would become a prey, and your sons, who today have no knowledge of good or evil, they shall enter, and to them I will give it, and they shall possess it. No knowledge of good or evil. The same line that ran through the Garden, the line of accountability, runs through the wilderness. The under-twenty had not been given the knowledge yet. The accountability did not attach.

The same age cutoff shows up elsewhere in God’s economy. In Exodus 30:11-16, when God instituted the half-shekel ransom that every Israelite was to bring as a covering for his soul, He set the age threshold at twenty: everyone who passes over to those who are numbered, from twenty years old and above, shall give a contribution to ADONAI. Twenty was the age at which a man was counted in the census, called into the responsibility of the community, accountable for his own ransom. Twenty was the age of majority in God’s order.

So here is what God did at Kadesh. The generation who heard His report, weighed it against the man-report, and chose the man-report, was held to account. The under-twenty, who had not yet been positioned to weigh the reports, were not. The accountable generation fell in the wilderness. The under-twenty wandered with them. They bore the consequence of a choice they did not personally make. They suffered the wandering they did not earn. But God preserved them through it, and on the other side He gave them the land their parents had refused.

Sit with that. The wandering itself was a kind of curse the under-twenty inherited. They did not vote for it. They were carried into it by the generation above them, the same way every child is carried into the consequences of the choices made by the adults around him. They paid a real price in the dust for a refusal that was not theirs. And yet the mercy held. He kept them alive. He fed them with manna. He spoke to them at every step. He brought them to the edge. When the parents had finally all died out, the under-twenty had come of age. They were now the accountable generation. The decision the older generation had refused was now theirs to make. And they made it. They crossed the Jordan. They took the land.

Now hold this against your own life.

Most of us did not consciously choose the I’m just a sinner saved by grace line. We received it. It was handed to us. It came from teachers we trusted, in congregations we loved, in a generation that had received it from the generation above them, with the warmth of inherited tradition and the apparent humility of received doctrine. We were the under-twenty when the inheritance arrived. The line came down from above and we drank it in because that was what was offered, and we had no knowledge of what we were drinking. We did not weigh the report. The report was the air we were breathing.

But we did inherit the wandering.

The struggle in your actual life, the gap between Scripture’s promises and the life you actually wake up to, the discouragement, the bitterness toward God that crept in when His promises seemed to fall flat, the sense that the New Testament was describing someone else’s life. That was the wandering. It looked like the curse of the older generation, and in a sense it was. You walked in the wilderness because the generation above you refused to walk in. You bore the consequence of an agreement with the ten that you never personally made. And yet the mercy held the whole time. He preserved you. He kept you alive in the dust. He fed you with His Word along the way, even when you did not know what you were eating. He brought you to the edge.

Reading this piece is the edge.

By the time you have walked through Numbers 13:33, through the grasshopper conclusion and the false slave identity and the manna teaching and the Tisha B’Av long-arc accountability pattern, by the time you have sat with the kainē ktisis of 2 Corinthians 5:17 and the according to your faith of Matthew 9:29 and the cross at the center of every reframe, you have been given knowledge of good and evil about who you actually are in Messiah Yeshua. You now know what you did not know before. You can no longer say the report was the air I was breathing. The two reports are on the table in front of you. Joshua and Caleb have spoken. The land has been named.

You have come of age.

Hear what this means. Before this piece, you wandered because you had inherited unbelief about your identity. The curse fell on you as inherited consequence and the mercy held because the accountability had not yet attached. After this piece, the line has moved. You are no longer the under-twenty. You are now standing where the parents at Kadesh stood. You have heard both reports. The Holy Spirit has shown you what He showed Joshua and Caleb. The next walking you do is your own walking, and it will carry the weight that the previous walking did not.

This is not a threat. This is a doorway. The same Holy Spirit who preserved you in the wandering is now extending to you the invitation that was offered to the parents at Kadesh and refused. The land is here. The new identity is here. The promise is here. You are at the edge of it. The choice now is yours, in a way the choice before was not.

And the God who set the line at twenty has spoken to those at the line. This is the most famous sentence Moses ever uttered to a generation at the edge of the land:

(Deuteronomy 30:19)

Choose life. Choose faith. Choose the report Joshua and Caleb brought. Choose the identity He spoke over you in Messiah. The under-twenty you came in as is not who you are anymore. You are now of the generation who makes the choice.

How God Humbled a People Who Thought They Were Already Humble

There is an insight hiding in plain sight in Deuteronomy that I missed for years, until I finally saw it in the last couple of weeks. Moses is rehearsing the wilderness journey to the people just before they cross into the land. He says this:

“You shall remember all the way that ADONAI your God has led you these forty years in the wilderness, to humble you and to test you, to know what was in your heart… And He humbled you and allowed you to hunger, and fed you with manna which you did not know, nor did your fathers know, so that He might make you know that man does not live by bread alone, but by every word that proceeds out of the mouth of ADONAI does man live.”

(Deuteronomy 8:2-3)

Stop. Sit with that.

These people were slaves last week. They lived under the slaver’s whip. They ate the bread of affliction. By any Western definition of humility, they were already humble. They were carrying the plundered wealth of Egypt in their packs, but they owned no land, no status, no power that they had built for themselves. What humbling could God possibly be talking about?

And yet God says, I humbled you. I fed you with manna so I could humble you.

What was the pride?

It was the pride of the slave’s identity. It was the pride of I know what kind of creature I am, and a slave is what kind of creature I am. It was the same false humility we have been talking about. It was the grasshopper-conclusion in a different costume. It was I’m just a sinner saved by grace worn by a people who had never even heard the gospel yet.

A people who insist on calling themselves slaves after God has called them His own are not humble. They are proud. They are placing their definition of themselves above His definition of them. They are holding their old story so tightly that God cannot lift them into the new story without first prying their fingers off the old one. The manna was the prying.

How did the manna humble them?

It broke the illusion that they were the providers of their own lives. They woke up every morning and discovered that bread had appeared on the ground because their God had put it there. They did not plant it. They did not work for it. They did not earn it. They received it because He gave it. They had to gather it daily. They could not stockpile it (Exodus 16:19-20). They had to depend on Him for the very next meal, every single day, for the rest of their wilderness journey. Daily bread. The Lord’s prayer is rehearsing the same lesson.

Here is the move that breaks the whole thing open. The manna was Yeshua. He said so Himself.

“I am the bread of life. The one coming to Me will never hunger, and the one believing in Me will never thirst… I am the living bread that came down out of heaven. If anyone eats of this bread, he will live forever.”

(John 6:35, 51)

The bread God rained down on the wilderness was the foreshadow. He was teaching them the shape of what He was going to give them in His Son. Not bread you grew in your own field. Bread that came from heaven, given to you, the very person of your Provider made edible and received by you in faith.

This is the humbling. This is the actual humbling.

Real humility is not pretending you are smaller than you are. Real humility is receiving who He says you are, accepting that He is the one who feeds you, accepting that He is the one who defines you, accepting that He is the one who fights for you. Real humility says yes, I am the new creation You made me. I did not earn it. I receive it. I choose You as the source of who I am. I purpose and choose for my will to submit to the truth You speak over me.

Yeshua said it again in the Sermon on the Mount. He pointed to the lilies of the field. They do not toil. They do not spin. And yet not even Solomon in all his glory was clothed like one of them (Matthew 6:28-29). The lilies do not produce their own beauty. They receive it from the Father who made them. That is their nature. That is their dignity. That is their humility. They live from what He gives.

And Paul said it even more starkly: while we were still sinners, Messiah died for us (Romans 5:8). The new identity was given before any of us did one thing to deserve it. The pride is in refusing it. The humility is in receiving it.

So when you cannot bring yourself to call yourself a new creation, when the words feel arrogant in your mouth, hear this honestly: the discomfort is not humility. It is the slave’s pride dressed up in religious clothing. The humble thing to do is to let Him define you, even when His definition outstrips anything you have produced. Lord, I believe. Help my unbelief (Mark 9:24). That confession, even spoken in trembling, is the beginning of the lift. Humble yourselves in the sight of the Lord, and He shall lift you up (James 4:10).

The lifting is on the other side of the receiving.

What You Believe About Yourself Is What You Get to Live

Yeshua said this directly. There was a moment when two blind men came to Him asking to see, and He asked them if they believed He could do it. They said yes. Then He said this:

“According to your faith let it be to you.”

(Matthew 9:29)

What you believe about yourself opens the door to what you get to inhabit of what is actually true about you. The new creation is real whether you believe it or not. But whether you can live from it, whether you can walk in it day by day, depends on whether you have started believing it.

Paul says the same thing in a different way:

“Likewise you also, reckon yourselves to be dead indeed to sin, but alive to God in Messiah Yeshua our Lord.”

(Romans 6:11)

The word translated reckon is the Greek word logizomai (Strong’s G3049). It is the word a bookkeeper uses. It means count it. Add it up. Treat it as the truth in your books. Paul is not telling the Romans to pretend. He is telling them to start writing the truth in the right column. Messiah already made you dead to sin. Your job is to count it that way in your own books. What you count yourself to be is what you get to live from.

Solomon said the same thing in older words:

“For as he thinks in his heart, so is he.”

(Proverbs 23:7)

The wisdom literature, the Gospels, and the letters of Paul are saying the same thing across a thousand years. What you reckon yourself to be is what you walk into the room as. The rope is not pulling the elephant back. The story the elephant tells himself about the rope is pulling the elephant back.

I am going to bring in a modern voice here for a moment, because the principle Yeshua and Solomon named is so robust that you can see it operating in places where Scripture is not even being read. Tony Robbins, who has spent decades studying human behavior at the highest levels with athletes, presidents, business leaders, and people in every kind of condition, says it this way. He says the strongest force in the human psyche is the need to stay consistent with how we define ourselves. He says a belief is a feeling of absolute certainty about what something means. And he says the most important move a person can make to change their life is to change the lies they have been believing about themselves and start living from a more accurate truth about who they actually are.

That is Yeshua’s principle dressed in different language. According to your faith be it unto you. You live whatever you hold with certainty. The mechanism does not care whether the certainty is true. It only cares whether you hold it with conviction. The mechanism will produce in your life whatever you have agreed to with the most belief.

This is one reason people who win the lottery so often end up worse off five years later than they were before they won. The money came. The identity did not change. Follow-up studies of large-prize winners have tracked bankruptcy rates running well above the general population within a few years of the win. They had more money to live the same script. Their money obeyed their identity, exactly the way Yeshua said it would.

The same thing is happening in the spiritual life. The promises are real. The kingdom is at hand. The believer is seated with Messiah in heavenly places. We have been given authority. The inheritance is sitting there waiting to be walked into, and most of us are still living like grasshoppers because our certainty about who we are has not caught up to what God has said about us. The bottleneck is not the inheritance. The bottleneck has always been identity.

Here is a small thing I want you to notice about how I read sources. Anything any of us encounters that is true, in psychology, in behavioral science, in the wellness world, in leadership research, in the arts, traces back to God. He is the Author. Tony Robbins did not invent the identity principle. Yeshua named it long before any modern teacher said a word. Whenever I bring in a modern teacher in a piece like this, I am doing two things at once. I am honoring what they articulated, and I am pointing you back to the original Source. He is the one who wired all of this. He gets the credit. Every good gift and every perfect gift is from above, coming down from the Father of lights, with whom there is no variation or shadow due to change (James 1:17). The researcher discovered the wiring. The Father wove it into reality. Both are real. The Father came first. The enemy works hard to convince us, especially our young people, that truth and beauty live somewhere other than in our Father’s house. They do not. Every true thing any of us has ever loved out there in the world is His. Trace it home.

The Enemy Who Wants You to Keep the Old Conclusion

Paul writes this about people who do not yet believe:

“The god of this age has blinded the minds of those who do not believe, lest the light of the gospel of the glory of Messiah, who is the image of God, should shine on them.”

(2 Corinthians 4:4)

The work of the enemy on the unbeliever is to keep him from seeing what Messiah has done. But for the believer, the enemy adapts. He cannot undo what Messiah did. He cannot uncrucify the old self. He cannot un-pass the things that have passed. So he works on what the believer believes about it all.

He whispers, you are still a sinner. He brings up the failure of last Tuesday. He produces evidence from yesterday afternoon. He says, look at what you did. That is what you are. And many believers nod and agree with him and say, yes, I am just a sinner saved by grace. They say it with what sounds like humility. And they have just put the rope back on themselves.

This is why Paul says:

“Casting down imaginations and every high thing that exalts itself against the knowledge of God, bringing every thought into captivity to the obedience of Messiah.”

(2 Corinthians 10:5)

The imaginations we cast down are not always about other people. Most of them are about ourselves. The most dangerous imaginations we have to cast down are the ones in our own head that contradict what Messiah has already done in us.

If we keep agreeing with the enemy about what we are, the gospel becomes inert in our daily lives. Paul puts the logic this way:

“Shall we who died to sin live any longer in it?”

(Romans 6:2)

His question only makes sense if we actually died to sin. If sin is no longer who we are, then living in it is a contradiction. If sin is still who we are, then Paul’s question is nonsense. The whole argument of Romans 6 depends on the believer accepting his new identity as the actual location of his being. A gospel that leaves us as sinners is a gospel that did not do its work. A half-cross. A wet ritual at baptism that changed nothing about the inside of the creature being dunked (or, in a line I could not resist borrowing from Monty Python, “some farcical aquatic ceremony”), instead of the real burial-and-resurrection it is supposed to be. That is not the gospel the New Testament announces.

The New Testament announces a complete cross, a real death, a real burial, a real resurrection, and a new creature on the other side of all of it.

The Lie That Sounds the Most Holy

James names the posture that wrecks the inheritance:

“But let him ask in faith, with no doubting, for he who doubts is like a wave of the sea driven and tossed by the wind. For let not that man suppose that he will receive anything from the Lord; he is a double-minded man, unstable in all his ways.”

(James 1:6-8)

The Greek word James uses for double-minded is dipsychos (Strong’s G1374). It means literally two-souled. The two-souled person holds two opposite stories about himself at the same time. He says with his mouth I am a new creation in Messiah Yeshua. He says in his interior monologue I’m just a sinner saved by grace. Both stories are running in his head all the time. Which one does he live from? Whichever one is louder in the moment. He oscillates. He is a wave. He receives nothing.

This is the posture the enemy wants. He does not need a believer to deny the gospel. He only needs a split on the question of identity. As long as the believer is two-souled about who he is, he is unstable in all his ways and unable to receive what God has already given him.

The I’m just a sinner saved by grace talk sounds humble. It is the lie that sounds the most holy. Who am I to claim I am a new creation? Better to call myself a sinner. That is the humble thing.

It is not humility. It is the inverse of humility.

Real humility receives what God has done. Real humility says, He called me a new creation, so I am a new creation, no matter what my feelings or my failures suggest in any given moment. Real humility submits to God’s account of who you are above your own account.

False humility puts your own diagnosis above God’s declaration. It says, in effect, I know better than what He says about me. It is pride wearing the costume of humility. It is calling God a liar about His own work in you, while sounding godly doing it.

Brené Brown captures the same truth in different language. She says: don’t puff up, don’t shrink, just stand your sacred ground. True humility is not the small posture. True humility is the accurate posture. It is standing exactly where God has set you, no taller and no shorter than the truth He has spoken over you. You are not puffing up when you say I am a new creation in Messiah Yeshua. You are standing on the ground God Himself put under your feet. Anything less than that is not modesty. It is leaving the sacred ground He gave you and stepping back into the dust.

The double-mindedness that produces it is the same unbelief that kept a generation out of the land. The grasshoppers spoke carefully. Their argument sounded sober, realistic, modest. We saw the giants. We were just grasshoppers. We have to be honest about our limitations. It sounded humble. It was unbelief. Plain and simple.

Like our elephant, the two-souled believer is held by a conclusion. He could walk away. He does not.

Walking the Way

There is a detail in the book of Acts that most readers walk past. Before the followers of Yeshua were called Christians, they were called by another name. Those of the Way (Acts 9:2, 19:9, 19:23, 22:4, 24:14, 24:22). Saul of Tarsus, before he became Paul, went out to arrest people he described as any who were of the Way, men or women. Paul himself, after his transformation, defended himself before Felix by saying according to the Way which they call a sect, so I worship the God of my fathers (Acts 24:14). The earliest identity of the believers in Yeshua was not a doctrine and not a denomination. It was a way of walking. Yeshua had told them what He was: I am the Way, the Truth, and the Life (John 14:6). They walked behind Him, and that walking became the name they were known by.

The Hebrew makes this even sharper than the Greek. The root verb is halakh (הלך, Strong’s H1980), one of the most frequent verbs in the Hebrew Scriptures. It means to walk, to go, to proceed, to live one’s life as a sustained motion in a chosen direction. The present-tense forms, holech (masculine, one walking) and holechet (feminine, one walking), appear constantly in everyday Hebrew, and not just in religious contexts. To be alive is to be walking. To follow God is to be walking with Him. Enoch walked with God (Genesis 5:24). Noah walked with God (Genesis 6:9). God told Abraham walk before Me and be blameless (Genesis 17:1). Micah asks what the LORD requires, and answers to do justly, to love mercy, and to walk humbly with your God (Micah 6:8). The Shema instructs us to talk of His words when you walk by the way (Deuteronomy 6:7). Walking is the framework of the whole obedient life.

From the same root comes the noun halacha (הלכה), which means literally the walking. In rabbinic Judaism it is the body of teaching on how to walk out the commandments in daily life. Christians often translate it as Jewish law and read it as legal restriction. The literal sense is different. Halacha is the path one walks. The Torah told you the direction; halacha was the practical conversation across centuries about how to actually take the steps. The whole tradition assumed that the obedience God wanted was an obedience in motion, on the road, with the community, in real time.

A first-century rabbi did not lecture from a chair. He walked from town to town and his talmidim (his disciples) walked behind him. Pirkei Avot 1:4 captured the discipleship posture in a single image: cover yourself in the dust of your rabbi’s feet. You were so close behind him on the road that his footsteps kicked up dust into your face. You watched his life from the back of his head all day. You listened to what he said to passersby. You observed how he ate, how he prayed, how he answered fools, how he honored women, how he treated children, how he handled accusation, how he carried weariness, how he laughed. You absorbed it by proximity. And take the image of closeness in for what it actually is. The dust on your face was not poetic. It was literal. Your rabbi’s sandals were stirring up the road and the road was settling on you. You walked home at the end of the day covered in him. That is how close he wanted you, and that is how close you wanted to be. The relationship was unembarrassed about that kind of proximity. It assumed it. It celebrated it. Then he sent you out to do it yourself.

This is exactly what Yeshua did. He called fishermen at their nets. He called a tax collector at his booth. He called a zealot named Simon. He did not select graduates of the rabbinical schools. The men around Him were not the most erudite candidates and not the most righteous-seeming candidates. He chose them as they were and walked with them as His talmidim. Follow Me (Matthew 4:19) is a Hebrew-rooted phrase: come walk behind Me, kick up the dust. Then He sent the twelve out two by two with authority over unclean spirits and instructions about what to take and what to leave behind (Mark 6:7-13). Later He sent out seventy more (Luke 10:1-12). They went on assignment. They came back. They debriefed. He walked with them between assignments and trained them on the road. He fed them. He answered their questions. He corrected them when they got it wrong. He sent them out again. The whole training method was on the way.

So what does this mean for you?

It means the new creation identity that has been the subject of this whole piece is not something you sit still and study yourself into. It is something you walk into. The Rabbi has called you. He has not waited for you to be ready. He has not asked you to graduate from rabbinical school first. He has not asked you to become erudite or righteous-seeming. He has spoken who you now are over your life and asked you to walk it out.

And hear what He is actually inviting you into. He could have spoken His will from heaven and left you to obey from a distance. He could have given you a holy book and expected you to figure it out alone. He chose instead the closest method human relationship has ever produced. He chose to walk with you. He chose to let you get His dust on your face. He chose this because He wants you that close. The dust-on-the-face image is not about humility or about you being small. It is about how close the Father wants to walk with His child. He has wanted this since the beginning. He walked with Adam in the cool of the day before there was anything to prove (Genesis 3:8). The Rabbi who says follow Me is the Father coming back into the garden looking for His friend.

Halakh. Come walk behind Me. The disciples were not ready when He called them. They became ready by walking. The man who denied Yeshua three times before the cock crowed became the man who preached the sermon at Pentecost on which three thousand were baptized. That was on-the-job training, on the road, with the Rabbi.

If you have been telling yourself you need to study more before you can do, you are half right. Studying without walking turns into paralysis and the quiet conviction that you are never quite ready. If you have been telling yourself you need to stop studying and just do, you are also half right, and you are also half wrong in a way I want to name directly. You never stop studying. The talmid does not graduate out of studying when he starts walking. He studies harder, because now the Rabbi is right there explaining things in real time. What changes is not whether you study. What changes is where the studying happens. You learn in motion. The road becomes the classroom. The Rabbi keeps unpacking what He has been showing you, only now He is doing it while you are doing the thing He sent you out to do. You walk. You do as you walk because He keeps sending you out and welcoming you back. You study as you walk because He keeps explaining as you go. You are mentored as you walk because His footsteps are kicking dust into your face the whole time. The doing and the studying are not opposed to each other. They are both subordinate to the walking.

An old African proverb names this whole posture in five words: while praying, move your feet.

The land is here. Now take a step.

He Is Showing You This Twice

There is a deeper layer in this Hebrew word I want you to see.

The singular form of the walking is halacha (הלכה). It ends in the letter hey (ה). The plural form is halakhot (הלכות), the daily rulings and specific steps of the walking. It ends in the letter tav (ת). The grammar itself is performing a move you may have seen before.

If you have walked through the yirat YHWH teaching with me elsewhere, you have already seen this exact pattern. YiraH (the freestanding noun for fear, ending in hey) becomes yiraT (the construct form, fear of, ending in tav) when it binds to YHWH. The standalone word ends with the letter of reveal (hey, the paleo-Hebrew picture of a man with arms raised, behold, look and see what is being shown). The bound word ends with the letter of covenant (tav, the paleo-Hebrew picture of two crossed sticks, the mark, the sign that seals). The grammar moves from the reveal to the covenant. The form names what is happening inside the word.

Now look at halacha → halakhot. Same letters. Same move. The singular halacha, the road we are taught, ends in the letter of reveal. The plural halakhot, the daily steps we actually take, ends in the letter of covenant. The walking is the reveal. The daily walkings are the covenant. The road as taught is something He shows you. The road as walked out is something you and He seal together, step by step.

I want you to notice what God is doing here.

He is the master storyteller. When He wants you to receive a truth that matters, He does not say it once and move on. He embeds the same move into the language itself, in word after word, so the careful reader who reads slowly catches the same revelation in different facets across the entire body of His Word. YiraH to yiraT in fear of the LORD. HalaCHA to halakhOT in the walking. Same letter shift. Same theological motion. Same invitation. He is riffing on a single truth across a thousand contexts. He is the artist who has been playing the same key signature in every song you have ever loved without you knowing it. He is the Father whose children come back night after night and say tell me that one again, and He tells it again, slightly differently, with the same ending, and the children laugh and clap because it is both familiar and new at once. That is the multiplication. That is the wonder. And you will see it again. He is not done showing you this.

G.K. Chesterton named this wonder better than I can, writing in Orthodoxy in 1908:

But perhaps God is strong enough to exult in monotony. It is possible that God says every morning, Do it again to the sun; and every evening, Do it again to the moon. It may not be automatic necessity that makes all daisies alike; it may be that God makes every daisy separately, but has never got tired of making them. It may be that He has the eternal appetite of infancy; for we have sinned and grown old, and our Father is younger than we.

You must become like a little child to enter the kingdom of God (Matthew 18:3). One of the things a little child does, that adults stop doing, is recognize a beloved story in a new shape and shout I know this one! The child does not get bored when the pattern returns. The child is delighted by the pattern returning. The same Father who told you who you are when you read 2 Corinthians 5:17 has been telling you who you are again in a Hebrew word whose grammar performs your new identity. He has been telling you this from the beginning and He has been telling you this in everything. The reveal becomes the covenant in word after word. The road becomes the daily walking step by step.

Hold this beside what is actually being asked of you. James writes that faith without works is dead (James 2:17). The Hebrew grammar has been saying the same thing all along. The walking that is only revealed and never taken is the hey without the tav. The walking that is revealed and then walked is the hey becoming the tav. Faith without works is the road as reveal only. Faith with works is the road as covenant. The covenant is in the steps.

This is the same choice the wilderness generation faced. The land had been revealed. The promise had been spoken. The hey had come. The next move was theirs. Were they going to seal it with their feet, with their tread, with their daily walkings into the land, or were they going to leave the reveal unsealed and let it die with them? They chose to leave it unsealed. They walked their refusal out into the wilderness instead of walking the promise out into the land. Life or death. Victory or defeat. The choice is which walking you do with your feet, not which feeling you have in your heart.

Your Rabbi has called you. He has revealed the road. He has spoken your new identity into existence. The call to adventure in every story you have ever loved is the same call He extended on the dusty road of first-century Galilee, and it is the same call He is extending to you now. Receiving the call is the hey. Walking the call is the tav. The covenant is in the steps.

He has shown you the same thing twice on purpose. Now walk it.

Two Stakes, One Surrender

Here is the truth that lives underneath everything I have written so far. You are going to surrender. Everyone surrenders. The question is not whether you will surrender. The question is which stake you will surrender to.

The elephant surrendered. He chose, somewhere in his young life, to surrender to the small story the rope first told him about who he was, and the surrender held for the rest of his life. He gave up the bigger life he was made for. He gave up the strength his body actually had. He gave up the distance his legs could have crossed. He surrendered the right to be the six-ton creature he actually was, and he traded it for the small circle a stake once defined. He surrendered to the lie. And the surrender produced his life.

You and I are doing the same thing every morning. We are surrendering to something. The question is to what.

Every day you can wake up and surrender to the stake the enemy drove into the ground when you were younger. To the voices that handed you a conclusion about who you are. To the I’m just a sinner saved by grace line you inherited without testing it. To the diagnosis your own flesh keeps offering you about your limits. That surrender is available, and it is the one many of us set as our default without noticing we are choosing it.

Or you can surrender to a different stake. The stake of the crucifixion. The cross Yeshua was secured to with three nails, which is the same letter tav the Hebrew language has been carrying as the sign of the covenant since before that letter ever became an alphabet. He surrendered to that stake on your behalf. He let Himself be defined by what He came to do for you. And on the other side of that surrender He bought you a new identity, sealed in His blood, named in His Word, walked out in His Spirit.

That is the surrender that produces life.

There is a beautiful weight to the word surrender. It is not the giving up of someone who has nothing left to give. It is the giving over of someone who has finally seen a better authority. Yeshua Himself modeled it on the night before His own crucifixion: not my will, but Yours be done (Luke 22:42). The Father’s will was always the better will. The surrender to that will was how His own assignment was completed. And He extends the same posture to you as relief, not as burden: take my yoke upon you, and learn of me; for I am meek and lowly in heart; and you shall find rest unto your souls. For my yoke is easy, and my burden is light (Matthew 11:29-30). The surrender is the rest.

You give up your right to define yourself. You let the One who made you tell you what kind of creature you actually are. You receive His definition over your own diagnosis. That is the surrender.

And here is the truth about your right to define yourself. You only ever had the option to surrender it well or to surrender it poorly. You never had the option to keep it. You are shaped by the choices you make in the free will He gave you, but He is still your designer. The only being in the universe with the authority to tell you who you are is the One who made you. Not you. Not your teachers. Not your therapist. Not your enemies. Not your friends. Not the algorithms feeding you content. Not the loudest voice in your own head. Only Him.

Notice how strong this becomes in Yeshua’s own teaching. When He asked the disciples who do you say that I am, Peter answered correctly: You are the Messiah, the Son of the living God (Matthew 16:16). Yeshua’s response is one of the most underread sentences in the Gospels: Blessed are you, Simon Bar-Jonah, for flesh and blood has not revealed this to you, but My Father who is in heaven (Matthew 16:17). Read that carefully. Even Peter’s correct recognition of who Yeshua was did not come from Peter’s own reasoning. It came as revelation from the Father.

If the correct revelation of who Yeshua is comes from the Father, how much more does the correct revelation of who you are come from the Father?

You cannot derive your identity from yourself any more than Peter could have derived his answer about Yeshua’s identity from himself. Both are revelation, and revelation comes from above. The Father has been speaking the truth about who you are since before you were knit together in your mother’s womb (Psalm 139:13). The new identity He gave you in Messiah Yeshua was His Word spoken over your life. Your job is to surrender to that Word. To receive it as revelation rather than to construct your own version of yourself in opposition to it.

So which stake will you surrender to?

The Word says that one day every knee will bow and every tongue confess that Yeshua Messiah is Lord, to the glory of God the Father (Philippians 2:10-11). Every one. The question on that day will not be whether you bowed. The question will be whether you bowed early enough that you got to walk with Him in the meantime. The choice now is the choice to bow today, and to keep bowing each morning as you walk this out. To bend the knee, lower the head, let the tongue confess that He is Lord of your life and that He alone can define you. To receive with gratitude and faith the Word He has already spoken over you. To trust the truth of what He did at the cross, and what He did at the resurrection, and what He keeps doing in you by His Spirit.

That is the surrender that sets the elephant free.

A Prayer to Walk This In

You have just read the verses. The truth is in front of you. The next step is to receive it in prayer.

Before we pray, we have to be honest about what we are repenting of. The pride underneath the I’m just a sinner story is wider and deeper than it first looks.

Is it pride to place our trust in teachings handed down to us by others without seeking His truth and His perspective for ourselves? Is it pride to presume to know? Is it pride to place our word above His? It is accepting and receiving the enemy’s lies dressed up in the costume of the traditions of men, which Yeshua said make the word of God of no effect (Mark 7:8-13). The just a sinner saved by grace line is one of those traditions. We did not invent it. We inherited it. Most of us never thought to question it. And by carrying it, we rendered the gospel ineffective in our own lives through unfaith and unbelief, often in simple ignorance.

That ignorance is not condemning. The mercy of God covers it. But continuing in it once we have seen it is a choice. The Holy Spirit has now shown us. We are accountable for what we have seen.

These dimensions of pride are some of the ones we have walked through together. Yours may include others. The Holy Spirit will bring to the surface what is yours to name. As you read this, He may already be bringing to mind specific teachers, specific traditions, specific moments, or specific lies you agreed with somewhere along the way. Bring what He brings. Specific repentance does what general repentance cannot. The prayer below leaves space for your specifics; do not skip past those moments.

The pattern below is the doorway out. It is from Wellspring Ministries (akwellspring.com), used by the Body for years to dismantle the agreements we made with the enemy and welcome in the truth that sets us free. I point you to them by name on purpose. If this prayer opens something you would rather not walk through alone, Wellspring offers one-on-one prayer ministry for exactly this kind of healing work, and I trust them. Reaching out for help like that is not a detour. It is part of walking the way.

One more thing before we pray. Speak these prayers out loud. Not silently in your head. The Word says: if you confess with your mouth Yeshua as Lord and believe in your heart that God raised Him from the dead, you will be saved. For with the heart one believes and is justified, and with the mouth one confesses and is saved (Romans 10:9-10). Confession is a heart-and-mouth move. The agreement you have been making with the enemy was made in your inner monologue; the breaking of that agreement gets spoken into the room where your own ears can hear it. Your own voice, hearing your own words of forgiveness and repentance and truth, is part of how this prayer does its work. Find somewhere private if you need to. Then speak it.

Forgive

Before we repent of anything, we forgive. Yeshua was direct about the order: if you forgive men their trespasses, your heavenly Father will also forgive you. But if you do not forgive men their trespasses, neither will your Father forgive your trespasses (Matthew 6:14-15). Forgiveness moves first. So we move first.

Heavenly Father, I purpose and choose to forgive whoever first handed me the line I’m just a sinner saved by grace as if it were the whole truth about me. I forgive the teachers, the pastors, the parents, the friends, the books, the songs, and the voices in the broader Body of Yeshua who carried this tradition forward, in good faith, and laid it on me without testing it against Your Word … (pause and name whoever specifically comes to mind right now. Speak the names out loud or to Him in your heart. He is listening.)

I also purpose and choose to forgive myself for receiving the line without questioning it, for choosing the smaller story over Your Word about me, for the years I have walked in agreement with something You never said about me. I release the debt of those who taught me this, and I release the debt I owe myself for accepting it. (Pause again. Anyone else? Any other source the Holy Spirit is bringing up right now? Forgive them too. Forgive yourself again if needed.)

I forgive in obedience to Your standard, knowing that my forgiveness of others opens the door to receiving Yours fully over me. (Matthew 6:14-15; Matthew 18:21-22; Mark 11:25)

Repent

Heavenly Father, I have agreed with the enemy about who I am. I have called myself just a sinner saved by grace when You called me a new creation in Messiah Yeshua. I see now that this story was a tradition of men, not Your truth, and that receiving it without testing it against Your Word was its own kind of pride. I repent of placing what others handed down to me above what You have said directly to me … (pause here and name the specific teachers, traditions, voices, or moments He is bringing to mind right now. Speak them out. He is listening.)

I repent of double-mindedness, of holding two stories about myself at the same time and letting whichever one was louder drive my life. I repent for rendering the gospel ineffective in my life through unbelief, often in simple ignorance … (pause again. Anything else? Specific lies you agreed with, specific moments you sealed the conclusion, anything He is putting His finger on right now? Name it.)

I purpose and choose to receive what You have said about me as more true than what I have been saying about myself. (Matthew 6:14-15; James 4:7-10; 1 John 1:9; Mark 7:8-13)

Cancel the Enemy’s Authority Over This

In the name of Yeshua and by the power of His blood, I cancel Satan’s authority over me in the matter of my identity. I cancel his right to whisper you are still a sinner in my ear. I cancel every agreement I have made with that lie, named and unnamed, remembered and forgotten. I close every door I gave the enemy through those agreements and through the traditions of men I received without testing. (Ephesians 4:27; James 4:7; 2 Corinthians 10:5)

Ask for Healing

Holy Spirit, heal the place in me that has been agreeing with the lie. Heal the shame that whispered I was still primarily a sinner. Heal the false humility that called Your finished work in me incomplete. Renew my mind to think about myself the way You think about me. Restore the parts of my heart, my conscience, and my imagination that the lie has occupied. (Psalm 103:3; Romans 12:1-2; Luke 4:18-19; Hebrews 9:14)

Listen

Before you ask, know this. He often speaks to each of us in the way He created us to hear. He is the one who designed your wiring. He knows whether you receive truth through pictures, through words, through impressions in your body, through a quiet inner sense, through a verse rising up in your memory, through emotion shifting, or through peace settling where pain used to be. He will speak in the modality you can actually receive in. Some people see. Some people hear. Some people feel. Some people simply know. None of these is more spiritual than the others. Give yourself permission to receive Him however He chooses to come. Faith is not insisting He speak in one particular way. Faith is trusting that He will speak, and receiving what He gives, exactly as it comes.

Now stop praying. Be quiet. Ask Him the question.

Holy Spirit, will You tell me Your truth about who I am? Tell me the truth that makes me free.

Listen.

(Stay here. Do not rush past this part. He may answer in a moment. He may take longer. You have not failed if nothing comes right away. He has time. So do you.)

Receive whatever He gives in the way He gives it. Write it down or speak it out loud so you do not lose it. Sometimes He answers in the moment you ask. Sometimes He answers tomorrow morning in a verse rising up, or through a friend, or in a dream, or in a song that catches you off-guard. The answer is His to give and yours to receive however and whenever He gives it. The truth He gives is the truth that displaces the lie. (John 14:26; John 16:13; Romans 8:16)

Walk Out Free

Yeshua said it plainly:

“If you abide in My word, you are My disciples indeed. And you shall know the truth, and the truth shall make you free… Therefore if the Son makes you free, you shall be free indeed.”

(John 8:31-32, 36)

Free indeed. Not partially free. Not free with one leg still tied to the stake. Free indeed.

You are no longer the elephant. The rope is not on you. The stake is not in the ground. You are no longer the captive of your own vain imaginations, or of the imaginations handed down to you by others. You are the new creation Messiah Yeshua made. He paid for you to walk in the freedom, the power, and the authority of who you actually are.

When the lie comes back tomorrow, and it will try, you do not have to fight it on its terms. You hold to two things. You remember what He told you in the Listen step, the rhema (Strong’s G4487), the specific spoken word He gave you in that quiet moment. And you stand on what His written Word has revealed about your identity, the truths we have walked through together in this piece, the logos (Strong’s G3056), the Scripture you can open and reread tomorrow morning. Both are essential. The Holy Spirit speaks new things, and the written Word anchors and confirms. The truth that made you free is still true, in both registers.

The road is still the classroom. The Rabbi is still walking ahead of you. You will learn as you go.

Walk it out.

A note for the reader on Strong’s numbers. The Greek words cited throughout this piece (kainos, ktisis, prōtos, hypotypōsis, logizomai, dipsychos) are each followed by a Strong’s reference number, like G2537 for kainos. Strong’s Concordance gives every Greek and Hebrew word in the Bible a unique number. If you want to look any of them up for yourself, sites like Blue Letter Bible (blueletterbible.org) and Bible Hub (biblehub.com) let you search by Strong’s number and see the word, every place it appears in Scripture, and the range of ways it has been translated. You do not need to read Greek to use it. The goal of including the numbers is not to send you to a classroom. It is to put the tools in your hand so you can verify or explore any of this on your own.
